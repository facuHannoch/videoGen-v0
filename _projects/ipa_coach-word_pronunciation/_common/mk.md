Rationale: 
I wanted to start making content in english, but I didn't like how I talked in english. It also annoyed me that the text to voice in the iPad didn't understand me when I wanted use that feature. After some research (don't remember how), I discovered the IPA alphabet, which is an alphabet for the sounds. Specifically learning english, if I learned the phonemes, and knew how to position the mouth, then I could simply use this and learn to speak in a much better way.
So I originally made an app that does that: takes a text in english and gives its transcription to IPA.
And I wanted to have users use this, that is why I made it public. But then I realized there were two problems with this:
First, it is just a tool, and people see little value in a simple tool like a translator
Second, it does not offer a guided program or a path to reach fluently. Even for myself, I had not a clear path: what do I do? Do I simply start pasting text?
And so I come up with this: a gamified and guided path to achieve fluency. For now focused on general american, thought it could be expanded for different accents later on.

Abstract problem:
I want to sound like a native.
Reaching native-like pronunciation is usually attempted through years of repetition or by living abroad. Both are slow, expensive, and unreliable.
**IPA makes pronunciation explicit. Instead of guessing how a word should sound, the learner can see the sound structure directly**.
The drawback of this, is that the person needs to learn a new alphabet. This is "theory" and it's learning 43 alphabbets. However, once these sounds are learned, then it's no longer repeating a single word dozens of times: you see the transcription, and then you know how to pronounce it. This has a second problem, which is that we know need to learn how the ipa transcription for each word. This could be a problem at first, but the brain would be decently quick at generalizing, and in the end is the same as hearing a single word multiple times, but it's a lot more efficient.

Product idea is: skip the years of immersion, learn the 43 sounds once, unlock any word.

## AI Conversation

**You already know state B.** You wanted to make content in English, but didn't like how you sounded. That's the target: *making content*, going on video, speaking publicly, being taken seriously. It's not abstract — it's "I want to open my mouth and not cringe."

**The iPad voice recognition failure is a perfect hook.** Concrete, relatable, embarrassing in a funny way. That's a TikTok opening right there.

**You've also diagnosed why the tool alone failed:** no path, no answer to "what do I actually do with this?" That's why the program mode exists — it turns a translator into a curriculum.

**The "????" in state B might actually be the user's own answer.** Different people have different reasons — content creation, job interviews, conversations with native speakers. Maybe you don't fill in that blank in the copy. You just name the feeling: *embarrassed by how you sound, even after years of English*.

One honest question: when you built this for yourself, did it work? Did the IPA approach actually move the needle on how you sound? Because if yes, that's the most powerful thing you can say — "I built this for myself, and here's what changed."

### Insights & Commentary

**The story is genuinely good.** Non-native speaker, frustrated by his own English, discovers IPA, builds a tool, realizes the tool isn't enough, builds a guided system. That arc is relatable, specific, and honest. It's not "we help people learn English" — it's a founder who lived the problem.

**The IPA angle is counterintuitive in a good way.** Most people associate IPA with linguistics nerds. Positioning it as a *shortcut* — "learn 43 sounds once, then any word is readable" — flips that perception. It sounds like a hack, not a textbook.

**The gap nobody talks about.** There are pronunciation apps (Elsa Speak), there are dictionary apps, there are English learning apps (Duolingo). Nobody is teaching the underlying sound system as the *foundation*. That's a real whitespace.

**What's weak:** The "I want to sound like a native" framing is overused and vague. I'd push toward something more specific — the moment of embarrassment, the concrete situation. "I wanted to record a video in English and I hated how I sounded" is 10x more powerful than "I wanted to improve my English."
